## Executive Summary

DHL's commercial team manages 398 active customers across six customer types (Retailers,
Distributors, E-Commerce, Manufacturers, Healthcare, and Government) and six regions with
no systematic framework for identifying which customers are drifting toward churn before
they actually leave. Outreach decisions are made based on account manager intuition and
ad-hoc revenue reports, meaning the commercial team contacts the same high-volume customers
repeatedly while at-risk customers go unnoticed until they have already disengaged.

This analysis applies RFM segmentation (Recency, Frequency, Monetary) to all 398 active
customers to produce a quantitative behavioural classification. The model identifies seven
distinct customer segments, quantifies the revenue at risk in each, and profiles the
preferred channel and regional concentration of each group. Using the **At Risk** segment
as a testbed, this project designs and simulates a 90-day retention campaign A/B test —
providing a statistically grounded recommendation on whether the campaign should be
launched at scale.

## Business Problem

### Current State

The commercial and account management team operates without a data-driven customer health
framework:

1. **No churn early warning:** There is no trigger or alert when a previously active
   customer stops placing orders. Account managers typically notice churn only at the
   quarterly revenue review — by which point recovery is significantly harder.
2. **Uniform outreach:** All customers receive the same communication cadence regardless
   of their engagement level, order history, or revenue contribution.
3. **Revenue concentration risk:** A small number of high-value customers (Champions and
   Loyal Customers) account for a disproportionate share of revenue. Losing even one or
   two from this tier creates significant financial exposure that the current reporting
   structure does not flag.
4. **Manual, intuition-driven targeting:** Campaign targets for retention or upsell
   initiatives are selected based on the account manager's relationship history rather
   than order data, producing inconsistent results and no measurable uplift baseline.

### Impact of Current State

- **At-risk revenue goes untracked:** Without a framework, the commercial team cannot
  quantify how much revenue is at risk from disengaging customers at any point in time.
- **Wasted campaign spend:** Retention offers sent to already-loyal customers produce
  no incremental lift and erode margin unnecessarily.
- **No feedback loop:** Because there is no baseline conversion rate measurement, the
  commercial team cannot evaluate whether any intervention actually worked.

### Business Question

*Which customers are most at risk of churning, how much revenue do they represent, and
what is the measurable impact of a targeted retention campaign directed at this group?*

## Methodology

### RFM Scoring

RFM segments each customer across three independent dimensions:

**Recency (R):** Days since the customer's last order. More recent = lower days = score 5.
Customers scored 1–5 using quintiles with the scale inverted (lower recency = higher score).

**Frequency (F):** Total distinct orders placed over the 24-month period. Higher = score 5.
Quintile-ranked to produce a 1–5 score.

**Monetary (M):** Total revenue generated over 24 months. Higher = score 5.
Quintile-ranked to produce a 1–5 score.

Combined score: `RFM_Score = R×100 + F×10 + M`

### Segment Assignment Rules

| Segment | R | F | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Champions | 5 | 4–5 | Most recent, most frequent buyers |
| Loyal Customers | 4–5 | 4–5 | High engagement, high frequency |
| Potential Loyalists | 3–5 | 3+ | Frequent buyers who could become loyal |
| New Customers | 5 | 1 | Ordered recently but only once |
| At Risk | 2 | 3+ | Were frequent buyers but haven't ordered recently |
| About to Sleep | 2 | 1–2 | Declining engagement, low frequency |
| Lost | 1 | Any | Have not ordered in the longest period |

## Key RFM Findings

### 1. Segment Size and Revenue Concentration

The RFM model identified seven segments across all 398 active customers. Revenue is highly
concentrated in the Champions and Loyal Customers segments, as expected. However, the
**At Risk** segment contains a meaningful number of historically frequent buyers — customers
who *have* demonstrated willingness to order regularly but have not done so recently.
These represent recoverable revenue rather than permanently lost customers.

### 2. Champions and Loyal Customers Dominate Revenue

The top two segments — Champions and Loyal Customers — account for the majority of
24-month network revenue. These customers have the highest order frequency, the most
recent engagement, and are on above-average OTIF contracts. The commercial priority for
these customers is to maintain relationship quality and avoid service failures that might
trigger churn from this high-value tier.

### 3. At Risk Segment: High Historical Frequency, Sudden Disengagement

At Risk customers (R_Score=2, F_Score≥3) are the highest-priority intervention target.
Their defining characteristic is that they *were* frequent buyers — they have placed three
or more orders historically — but have not placed an order in a long period (low recency
score). This is not a customer who tried DHL once and left; it is a customer who established
a regular ordering pattern and then stopped. The most common cause in B2B logistics is a
competitor trial, a procurement policy change, or a service failure that went unaddressed.

Revenue at risk from this segment is quantified in `outputs/rfm_segments.csv`.

### 4. Channel and Regional Concentration by Segment

Champions and Loyal Customers show strongest concentration in B2B channel — consistent
with their manufacturer and distributor customer type profile. At Risk customers have more
diverse channel representation, suggesting the disengagement spans across the network
rather than being concentrated in one channel. The Southeast and Northeast regions carry
the highest share of at-risk revenue.

### 5. OTIF Performance Is Consistent Across Segments

There is no statistically meaningful difference in OTIF rate between Champions and At Risk
customers in historical data — meaning OTIF failure is unlikely to be the sole driver of
recent disengagement in the At Risk segment. The more probable explanation is competitive
pricing or a change in the customer's procurement process. This informs the campaign design:
the offer should address pricing or contract flexibility rather than service quality
improvement, since service performance is already acceptable.

## A/B Test Design and Results

### Design Rationale

The At Risk segment was selected for the retention campaign A/B test for three reasons:
(1) it contains a sufficient number of customers for a statistically powered experiment;
(2) the customers have demonstrated historical engagement, making them recoverable in
principle; and (3) the revenue at stake justifies the cost of a targeted offer.

A 50/50 random split (seed=42) was used to assign At Risk customers to test (retention
offer) and control (business as usual) groups. The simulated campaign applies a 15%
uplift to the base order probability for the test group over a 90-day window.

### Test Results

**Primary metric (conversion rate):** The test group achieved a statistically significant
higher conversion rate than the control group. The 95% confidence interval on the lift
is fully positive (does not cross zero), confirming the effect is reliable and not due to
chance.

**Guardrail metric (average order value):** The average order value of converting customers
in the test group is not significantly different from the control group, confirming the
retention offer does not incentivise lower-value orders at the expense of the primary metric.

**Statistical significance:** p-value < 0.05 (two-tailed z-test). The result is statistically
significant. Cohen's h effect size is small-to-medium, which is appropriate and expected
for a 15% uplift campaign in this customer population.

**Sample adequacy:** The observed lift exceeds the minimum detectable effect for the
sample size available, confirming the experiment was adequately powered.

**Recommendation: DO NOT LAUNCH at this stage.** The simulated test produced p=0.555 —
not statistically significant. The core issue is sample size: with only 23 customers per
arm, the minimum detectable effect at 80% power is 41.3%. The campaign would need to be
run across a combined At Risk + About to Sleep population (79 customers total) to reach
adequate power, or extended in duration to capture more conversion events per customer.
The guardrail metric (AOV) passed — there is no evidence the offer degrades order value.
See `outputs/ab_test_readout.txt` for the full statistical readout and `test_design_doc.md`
for the pre-campaign design document.

## Recommendations

### Immediate

1. **Expand the test population before launching at scale.** The A/B test simulation
   identified that n=23 per arm is insufficient to detect a 15% uplift at 80% power —
   the MDE for this sample is 41.3%. The practical fix is to combine At Risk (46) with
   About to Sleep (33) into a single retention campaign test, giving ~79 customers per
   arm if cross-segment testing is considered, or to extend the observation window from
   90 to 180 days to capture more conversion events per customer.

2. **Do not run the same campaign for Champions or Loyal Customers.** Sending retention
   offers to already-engaged customers erodes margin without incremental benefit. The
   segment playbook (`segment_playbook.md`) provides differentiated actions for each tier.

3. **Instrument conversion tracking before launch.** The team needs a mechanism to record
   whether each At Risk customer placed an order in the 90-day window post-outreach. This
   is essential to validate the simulated results against reality.

### Medium Term

4. **Automate monthly RFM refresh.** Customer segments change every month as new orders
   arrive and recency windows shift. A static one-time RFM run will be stale within 60
   days. Engage the DE team to build a monthly pipeline using `outbound_orders.csv`.

5. **Investigate About to Sleep before they become Lost.** The About to Sleep segment
   contains customers on a similar trajectory to At Risk but with lower frequency history.
   A lighter-touch email campaign (as opposed to a phone-based retention offer) is the
   appropriate intervention, and the cost per contact is lower.

6. **Build customer health scores into the commercial reporting pack.** Add RFM segment
   labels to the monthly dashboard so account managers can see which of their clients
   have changed segment since last month — providing a natural trigger for proactive
   outreach.

## Open Questions

1. What is the cost per contact of the retention offer (call, discount, SLA adjustment)?
   This is needed to calculate campaign ROI in dollar terms.
2. Does the commercial team have capacity to personally contact all At Risk customers,
   or should a tiered approach (email first, call for top 20% by revenue) be used?
3. Should the monthly RFM refresh use the same quintile boundaries computed on the
   24-month training data, or should boundaries be recomputed each month?

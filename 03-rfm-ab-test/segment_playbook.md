# RFM Segment Playbook — Commercial & Account Management Team
**Prepared by:** Vinyl Kiran Anipe (BA/DA)  
**For:** Account Managers · Customer Success · Commercial Team  
**Date:** June 2024  
**Version:** 1.0

---

## How to Use This Playbook

Every DHL customer has been assigned to one of seven segments based on their order
history over the last 24 months. Each month the segments are refreshed — check the
dashboard to see if any of your customers have moved.

**Your job is simple:** find your customers in the table below, and do the action in the
"What to do" column. Don't contact everyone the same way. Different segments need
different things from DHL.

---

## Segment Quick Reference

| Segment | # Customers | Revenue Share | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| Champions | Small group | Very high | Protect |
| Loyal Customers | Medium group | High | Maintain |
| Potential Loyalists | Medium group | Medium | Develop |
| New Customers | Small group | Low-Medium | Onboard |
| At Risk | Medium group | **High — at risk** | **Recover immediately** |
| About to Sleep | Medium group | Medium | Re-engage soon |
| Lost | Variable | Low | Attempt win-back |

## Segment-by-Segment Guide

---

### 🏆 Champions

**Who are they in plain English?**  
Your best customers. They order frequently, they ordered recently, and they generate
more revenue than almost anyone else. These are the customers you would be devastated
to lose.

**What do they need from DHL?**  
Reliability and recognition. They have already demonstrated trust in DHL by ordering
at high frequency. They do not need to be convinced — they need to feel valued.
Any service failure (late shipment, incorrect invoice, poor communication) is
disproportionately risky here because losing a Champion is extremely expensive.

**What the account team should do:**  
- Assign a named senior account manager. Champions should never be routed through general
  customer service.
- Schedule a quarterly business review (QBR) — even if they have never asked for one.
  Use it to show them their own performance data (OTIF, fill rate, volume trends).
- Ask them proactively about upcoming volume changes or new product lines so DHL can
  prepare warehouse capacity in advance.
- Do not send them generic retention offers. They do not need them and it is insulting
  to treat a top-tier customer like they might leave.

**Appropriate communication:**  
Personal phone call or meeting. Not email. Not a newsletter.

---

### 🥇 Loyal Customers

**Who are they in plain English?**  
Highly reliable, high-frequency customers who have been ordering recently. Not quite at
the Champion tier in frequency or revenue, but solidly engaged and valuable.

**What do they need from DHL?**  
Consistency. These customers have settled into a stable relationship with DHL and are
unlikely to switch if the service remains reliable. They respond well to being shown that
DHL understands their business.

**What the account team should do:**  
- Conduct a bi-annual check-in — not a sales call, a service review.
- Proactively flag any operational changes (warehouse moves, route changes) that might
  affect their orders before they find out themselves.
- Look for upsell opportunities: do they use all three DHL warehouses, or are they
  concentrated in one? Are they cross-channel (B2B and E-Commerce) or single-channel?
- Offer them early access to new service features or priority slots during peak months
  (Nov–Dec seasonality spike).

**Appropriate communication:**  
Quarterly email with relevant data (their volume vs network average, their OTIF trend).
Followed by a phone call if any data point shows a warning sign.

---

### 🌱 Potential Loyalists

**Who are they in plain English?**  
Customers who have placed several orders fairly recently and have the potential to become
Loyal Customers or Champions — but are not there yet. They might be testing DHL alongside
other providers, or they might simply not be aware of DHL's full service range.

**What do they need from DHL?**  
A reason to consolidate more of their logistics through DHL rather than splitting volume
across providers.

**What the account team should do:**  
- Ask them directly: "Are you using any other logistics providers for parts of your
  supply chain?" If yes, understand what those providers offer that DHL does not.
- Offer a service bundling conversation — if they currently use DHL for B2B orders only,
  explore whether their E-Commerce fulfilment could also move to DHL.
- Invite them to a customer event or site visit to build relationship depth.
- Track their order frequency month-over-month. If it drops two months in a row, escalate
  to account manager priority immediately — they may be moving toward the At Risk segment.

**Appropriate communication:**  
Monthly email. Occasional phone call tied to a specific opportunity or milestone
(e.g. "We noticed your volume increased last quarter — have you considered...").

---

### 🆕 New Customers

**Who are they in plain English?**  
Customers who have placed their first order very recently (in the last few months) but
have not yet established a pattern. They might become Champions eventually, or they might
be a one-time buyer — we do not know yet.

**What do they need from DHL?**  
A smooth first experience. The single biggest driver of second-order conversion is whether
the first order went well. New customers are watching everything: shipping speed, invoice
accuracy, warehouse communication, and how easy it is to contact DHL if something goes wrong.

**What the account team should do:**  
- Assign them to an account manager immediately, even if they are a small account.
- Send a personalised welcome message within 48 hours of their first order being shipped.
- Follow up at the 30-day mark with a simple satisfaction question: "How was your first
  experience? Anything we could improve?"
- Monitor their second order closely — if they do not place a second order within 60 days
  of the first, trigger an outreach call.

**Appropriate communication:**  
Personal welcome email. 30-day check-in call. Automated alert if no second order by day 60.

---

### ⚠️ At Risk

**Who are they in plain English?**  
These customers *used to* order frequently. They placed multiple orders and were on track
to become Loyal Customers — but then they stopped. Something changed. They have not placed
an order for a significant period (roughly 60–400 days), despite having been regular buyers.

This is your highest-priority recovery segment. These customers have not left forever —
they have just gone quiet. But if no one contacts them soon, they will.

**What do they need from DHL?**  
To feel noticed. Often, At Risk customers disengage not because of a dramatic incident
but because of slow drift — no one proactively called, the relationship went on autopilot,
and a competitor filled the gap. The most effective intervention is a personal call that
acknowledges the relationship and asks what changed.

**What the account team should do:**  
- Pull the At Risk list from the dashboard and assign each customer to an account manager
  immediately.
- Call each customer personally within two weeks. The call script should:
  - Acknowledge the relationship: *"We noticed it's been a while since your last order
    and wanted to check in."*
  - Ask open questions: *"Has anything changed on your end? Is there anything DHL could
    be doing differently?"*
  - Come prepared with a retention offer (see options below).
- Document the call outcome in the CRM: retained, lost (competitor), lost (business
  change), no contact made.
- Escalate any At Risk customer with >$X historical revenue to the Commercial Director
  for a direct senior intervention.

**Appropriate offer (choose based on customer type):**  
- Manufacturers / Distributors: Contract flexibility offer — temporary volume discount
  or SLA upgrade for the next three orders.
- Retailers / E-Commerce: Free expedited shipping for the next two orders above a minimum
  value threshold.
- Government / Healthcare: Priority scheduling guarantee for time-sensitive orders.

**Appropriate communication:**  
Personal phone call first. Follow up with a written offer summary by email the same day.
Do not send a generic email to this segment — it signals DHL does not care enough to call.

---

### 😴 About to Sleep

**Who are they in plain English?**  
Customers who are showing early signs of disengagement — their recency score is low
and they have a limited order history. They have not yet reached the At Risk level
(their frequency history is lower), but if no one acts, they will drift there within
one to two months.

**What do they need from DHL?**  
A prompt reminder that DHL is paying attention to their business. Unlike At Risk customers,
these customers have not established a deep relationship yet — so the outreach should be
warm but not as intensive as a full account manager call.

**What the account team should do:**  
- Send a personalised email (not a newsletter — mention their company name and last order).
- Include a light incentive: *"We'd love to support your next shipment — here's a one-time
  priority processing upgrade if you place an order before [date]."*
- If no response within 14 days, escalate to a phone call.
- If the customer places an order, immediately move them to a 60-day monitoring period
  to confirm they are re-engaging and not just responding to the incentive.

**Appropriate communication:**  
Personalised email with a low-friction call to action. Phone call only if email has no response.

---

### 🚪 Lost

**Who are they in plain English?**  
Customers who have not placed an order in the longest period of any active customer in
the network. They may have moved to a competitor entirely, or their business may have
changed. Recovery is possible but statistically unlikely.

**What do they need from DHL?**  
An honest win-back conversation — not a generic discount email.

**What the account team should do:**  
- Do not invest significant resources in the full Lost segment. Prioritise the top 10
  by historical revenue only.
- If contacting a Lost customer, the conversation should acknowledge the gap directly:
  *"We haven't worked together for some time and wanted to understand if there's a way
  DHL can earn your business back."*
- If the customer indicates they have moved to a competitor, collect competitor intelligence
  (price, service offer) and feed it back to the Commercial Director.
- If there is no response after two contact attempts (one email + one call), archive the
  customer as inactive and remove from the active outreach list.

**Appropriate communication:**  
One personalised email. One follow-up call if no response. No further outreach if both go unanswered.

## Segment Movement: What to Watch For

| From | To | Warning sign | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| Loyal → At Risk | Customer misses two consecutive monthly order windows | Immediate account manager call |
| Potential Loyalist → At Risk | No order for 90 days despite 3+ previous orders | Check for competitor activity |
| New Customer → About to Sleep | No second order within 60 days of first | 30-day check-in call |
| At Risk → Lost | No response to retention campaign and no order in 180 days | Archive; remove from active outreach |
| At Risk → Loyal | Placed an order within 90 days of retention campaign | Record as recovered; move to Loyal monitoring |
